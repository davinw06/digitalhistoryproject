1638 – Arrival of British logwood cutters

This marks the beginning of British settlement and extraction economy, prioritizing extraction over development. With the British rule rarely offering assistance in development, Belize would soon be left with little infrastructure.

1720s – Mahogany expansion

Early forestry regulations and trade restrictions were put in place, favoring British merchants, limiting local agriculture and development. To achieve this, they would have to exert their influence over the indigenous communities existing in Belize.

Late 1700s-1800s - British Crown formalizes control

This marks the period where development and agricultural restricting policies entrenched social and economic inequality, officially limiting local development and access to resources, leaving local communities as subject to stagnation by the British rule.

1838 – Emancipation

A period where moral backlash led to the legal emancipation of slaves, though slavery continued in Belize. This means that Belize would be further subjugated to the exploitation of its resources, further impacting the health of the environment.

1862 - British Honduras declared a Crown Colony

Appointing a Governor General, making previous policies set in place, a legal matter. These legal frameworks only work to further expand British economy, not Belize's economy.

Late 1800s – Peak mahogany exports

Resource extraction structured to benefit Britain; limited reinvestment in local infrastructure or diversified economy. This marks where mahogany logwood would soon reach its highest scarcity as a result of overcutting up to this point.
